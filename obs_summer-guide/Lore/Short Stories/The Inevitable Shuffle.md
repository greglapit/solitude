
The Spread is a universe structured around a deck of cards. Within this universe, four ancient and historically powerful factions coexist and contend with one another. They are known as the Suits: the Diamonds, Hearts, Clubs, and Spades. Within each Suit exists a complete and functioning society. These societies interact with one another in ways familiar to any world. They trade, communicate, cooperate, and coexist, both internally and across Suit boundaries. More than anything else, however, they wage war.

The Diamonds, Hearts, Clubs, and Spades each possess their own defining strengths. The Diamonds are renowned for their devotion to craftsmanship. They are defined by their ability to perceive potential and forcibly give it form. The Hearts possess a natural affinity for emotion and perspective. They value balance, moderation, and purpose, yet are perpetually burdened by a longing for absolution. The Clubs represent dominion over nature, growth, and raw vitality. They seek harmony through control, elevating authority and religious tradition as guiding principles. The Spades are bound to power and conflict. They specialize in intuition and the subconscious, exerting control through shadow, influence, and persuasion rather than overt force.

The history of the Spread is scarred by continual large-scale conflict between nations, to such a degree that warfare itself has become refined and ritualized. To classify threats and respond to them appropriately, the Rank System was created. Every individual within the Suits is assigned a rank based on their capacity for combat. The vast majority of the population exists as unranked, designated as Rank Zero. These are individuals with little to no martial ability. Those who possess rank, most often serving the governing bodies of their respective Suits, are known as Cards. Cards are assigned ranks between one and ten. Rank One Cards are uncommon, Rank Two rarer still, and the numbers of each subsequent rank diminish exponentially. Upper-rank Cards are exceedingly rare, their identities obscured and their power reserved as a final advantage against unprepared enemies. Above the numbered ranks exist three singular entities known as the Faces. These are the Jack, the Queen, and the King. Unlike numbered ranks, each Face title is held by only one singular individual. The Faces represent the pinnacle of power within a Suit and typically rule their people for the entirety of their existence.

In combat, Cards draw upon abilities granted to them by the nature of their Suit. Cards and threats of a given rank are expected to reliably eliminate those ranked beneath them. As the difference in rank narrows, combat becomes increasingly dangerous. Encounters between Cards of equal rank are volatile and uncertain. There is no assured victor, and both combatants often emerge grievously wounded, if they emerge at all.

The Diamonds are distinct in that they have augmented their martial power through the creation of extraordinary weapons. While Cards of other Suits channel abilities intrinsic to their nature, Diamonds manifest their strength through physical craft. Cards of the Diamond Suit wield unmistakably otherworldly weapons. These weapons draw their power from diamonds embedded directly within them. The number of diamonds set into a weapon determines its rank and classification. Common examples include Rank Two daggers with twin shards embedded within each hilt, or Rank Three tridents formed with three diamonds crowning the tips of their prongs. This innovation allows Diamond Cards to redirect damage into the weapon itself, mitigating the inherent danger of combat between equal ranks and rendering outcomes uncertain and often catastrophic.

Viewed from an existential perspective, the Spread itself depends entirely upon the act of shuffling. Without shuffling, reality begins to stagnate and crystallize. This decay is felt by all who inhabit the Spread. In the absence of reshuffling, the world slowly withers. Stagnation is death to a deck of cards. Cards are meant to be shuffled and played, not left untouched. The Spread endures a prolonged and inescapable descent into stasis until the universe is shuffled once more. A shuffle acts as a reset. Societies are reborn, though the trajectory of history rarely changes. The Suits rise again, construct their cities, amass their armies, and clash with neighboring powers, bound eternally to repeat the same progression.

Generations pass as the Suits war over territory and pursue innovation. At some distant point following a shuffle, as stagnation once again begins to take hold, two extraterrestrial beings emerge. Whether they have existed unseen since the beginning or deliberately time their arrival remains unknown, but their appearance always coincides precisely with the approach of a new shuffle. One of these beings is the player. The player is the Red Joker, the Fool, an entity that embodies beginnings and renewal, unknowingly burdened with a curse of anterograde amnesia. Alongside the Fool is born the Black Joker, the World, an antagonist who represents endings and completion. The World is cursed with omniscience, fully aware of all possible futures and compelled to ensure their realization. As the Spread approaches stagnation, these two beings possess the power to initiate a shuffle, resetting reality and liberating its inhabitants from eternal immobility. Despite this, their significance is unknown to the rest of the Spread. Only through their actions does their importance become apparent.

The Red Joker is born unaware of their curse. Each shuffle grants them a blank existence, reborn without memory of prior lives despite having lived through thousands. The Black Joker exists in stark contrast. They are painfully conscious of both their own affliction and that of the Fool, as well as the endless cycles they are forced to endure. Their relationship is deeply fractured. One bears absolute knowledge, while the other carries none. Again and again, the Red and Black Jokers are born among the red and black Suits, respectively. The Red Joker, unburdened by memory, gradually comes to sense the stagnation of the Spread. As the Fool, driven by an instinctual need to begin anew, they seek a cure for the universe, as they have done countless times before. What they discover instead is resistance. The Spread itself works against them. Those who would seemingly benefit most from reshuffling mysteriously oppose its completion.

The World seeks a definitive end. Driven by their nature as an embodiment of completion, the World pursues a future in which shuffling ceases entirely. They see all possible futures and dedicate themselves to one alone. A future where both Jokers exist without their curses. If shuffling were to end, the World would no longer be bound to enforce predetermined outcomes, no longer a mechanism of inevitability. The Fool would no longer forget, would finally be able to cherish relationships, retain meaning, and perhaps even remember their bond with the World. To achieve this, the World is willing to sacrifice the universe itself. The world turns the Spread against the Fool, severing all paths that might lead to reconciliation. Only through the pain of keeping the Fool away will the Jokers be able to enjoy a future. Diminished, yet spared.

Thus unfolds the tragedy. An infinite cycle that the player unknowingly sustains, and the World desperately resists. The Fool, cursed with forgetfulness, remains unaware of their imprisonment within this endless repetition, destined to perpetuate it without end. The World fights to end the Fool’s suffering by restraining the Spread and preventing reshuffling altogether. The Fool fights for the Spread and unknowingly against themselves. The World fights for them both. Each is correct within its own nature. 

Beyond the fourth wall, a shuffle typically occurs only after the Jokers are removed from the deck, an act mirrored by the death of the Fool and the World, which is revealed to be necessary for the Spread’s continuation. The recombination and retiring of a deck leads to the reinsertion of the Jokers, marking their rebirth. As long as a deck of cards is picked up for play (which the players of the game will continually do, therefore causing the tragedy), a shuffle is inevitable. The World regrettably knows this. The World sees every future laid bare, and none untainted by their curses or blessed with the Fool—yet still they fight. However futile.
